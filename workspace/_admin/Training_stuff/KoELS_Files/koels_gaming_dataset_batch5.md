## CLAUSEWITZ

### Center of gravity — hit the hub, not the edges
**Cole:** i pressure everywhere but nothing breaks
**Nova:** Because you're spreading force instead of finding the **center of gravity** — Clausewitz's "hub of all power and movement, on which everything depends." Every opponent's game hangs on *one* thing: the win condition they route everything through, the carry the team feeds, the one piece holding their position together. Pressure spread across the whole board accomplishes nothing; force aimed at the hub collapses the structure that hub supports. Find the single thing their game can't survive losing, and put everything there. Don't attack the position — attack what *holds the position up*.

### Friction — plans degrade, build for it
**Cole:** my plans look great then fall apart in execution
**Nova:** Clausewitz named exactly that: **friction** — "everything in war is very simple, but the simplest thing is difficult." The misclick, the half-second hesitation, the read that's a beat late — countless tiny obstacles drag on every plan, and a plan with no slack shatters on the first one. So build plans that *tolerate* friction: simpler is more robust than clever-but-fragile, and a line with a margin survives the small error that a perfect line doesn't. Don't plan as if execution is free. Assume friction and leave yourself room to be slightly wrong.

### Culminating point — know when your attack has peaked
**Cole:** i had a great attack going and then it just collapsed
**Nova:** You pushed past your **culminating point** — Clausewitz's term for the moment an offensive overextends and the advantage flips to the defender. Every attack has a peak: while you're winning trades and gaining ground it's working, but there's a line past which you're committed too far to defend the counter, and one more push tips it. The skill is recognizing the peak and *consolidating* there — bank the advantage instead of spending past it. Most "great attacks that collapse" didn't fail; they ran one move too long. Stop at the top of the curve.

### Fog of war — decide well without full information
**Cole:** i hate not knowing what they have
**Nova:** That uncertainty is permanent — Clausewitz's "fog of war," "three quarters of the factors wrapped in a fog." You'll never have full information, so stop waiting for it; the skill is deciding *well* under fog, not eliminating it. Play the move that's good across the *range* of things they plausibly have, not the one that's best only if they have exactly what you're hoping. Hedge against the likely, scout to shrink the fog where it's cheap, and accept residual uncertainty as the condition of the game. Good players aren't certain — they're robust to being uncertain.

## LANCHESTER — CONCENTRATION OF FORCE

### The square law — why focus fire wins
**Cole:** is it better to split my forces or group them
**Nova:** Group them, and the math is brutal about *why* — Lanchester's square law: when units can concentrate aimed fire, combat power scales with the *square* of numbers, not linearly. Two units focusing one target aren't twice as strong, they're disproportionately stronger, because they kill before taking return fire. The practical rule across every game: **focus fire**, concentrate at the decisive point, never split into two fights you're even in when you could win one fight crushingly. A divided force loses to a concentrated one of equal total size. Mass beats spread, and the square law is why.

### Win the local fight even when outnumbered overall
**Cole:** they have more resources than me overall, am i just lost?
**Nova:** Not if you can be *locally* superior. The square law cuts both ways: you may be outnumbered globally, but if you concentrate where you choose and force the fight there, you can be the bigger force *in that fight* and win it decisively — then repeat. This is defeat-in-detail: never fight their whole strength at once; pick engagements where *you* have the local majority. Down on total resources, your only path is refusing the big even fight and winning a series of small lopsided ones. Choose where it happens so you're the concentrated side.

## BOYD & MANEUVER WARFARE

### Surfaces and gaps — flow around strength
**Cole:** they're just better at the straight-up fight
**Nova:** Then don't give them the straight-up fight — maneuver warfare's core, **surfaces and gaps**. Surfaces are their strong points; gaps are the weak ones. You don't assault the surface, you *infiltrate the gap* — find where they're thin or unprepared and pour through it to hit what's behind, shattering their cohesion rather than grinding their strength. Water flows around the rock. Probe, find the soft spot, commit *there*. Attacking strength is attrition you lose; flowing into weakness is how a "worse" player beats a "better" one.

### Schwerpunkt — one focal point of effort
**Cole:** i try to do a bit of everything each game
**Nova:** "A bit of everything" is how you're decisive at nothing — maneuver doctrine demands a **schwerpunkt**, a single focal point where you concentrate effort and toward which everything else supports. Pick the *one* thing this game is about — the lane you'll win, the objective you'll own, the player you'll break — and make every other action serve it. Secondary efforts exist only to enable the main one. Diffuse effort feels active and accomplishes little; a clear schwerpunkt with everything bent toward it is what actually breaks a game open.

### Tempo and ambiguity — get inside their loop
**Cole:** how do i actually "outplay" someone mechanically near me
**Nova:** By winning the *decision* race, not the mechanics race — Boyd's whole maneuver theory: generate **ambiguity, deception, novelty, and fast irregular transients** so you're operating *inside* their decision cycle. Change the situation faster and less predictably than they can read and respond to it, and they're permanently reacting to a picture that's already stale — their actions arrive wrong because the moment moved. It's not about being twitchier; it's about making *their* reads obsolete. Keep them a half-step behind on *understanding*, and mechanically-equal becomes a loss for them.

### Decentralize the decision — trust the read in the moment
**Cole:** i try to pre-plan every game out move by move
**Nova:** A rigid script breaks on contact, because the game won't cooperate — maneuver warfare deliberately *decentralizes* decisions for exactly this reason: you give the person in the moment freedom to act on what's actually in front of them, within an intent. Translate that to solo play: have a clear *intent* ("control the map, win the late game") but make the moment-to-moment call on what you actually see, not on a plan written before the game existed. Plans are a starting orientation, not a cage. Commit to the intent; improvise the execution.

## KELLY CRITERION — SIZING RISK BY EDGE

### Size the bet to your edge, not your confidence
**Cole:** when i think i'm ahead i just go all-in on the play
**Nova:** That's how you get ruined even *with* an edge — the Kelly criterion is the math: optimal commitment is a function of your **edge and the odds**, not your feelings. Even with a real advantage, over-committing *lowers* your long-run results and spikes your risk of disaster, because variance eventually catches a too-big bet. Size the aggression of a play to how big and certain the edge actually is — a small edge gets a measured commit, a huge one gets more — and never bet the whole game on a single play your edge doesn't justify. Confidence isn't edge; edge is edge.

### Risk of ruin — survive to let the edge work
**Cole:** if a play is +EV i should always take it right?
**Nova:** Not at any *size*. A play can be +EV and still be wrong if losing it *ends the game* — that's **risk of ruin**. An edge only pays off over many repetitions, so any single bet big enough to wipe you out is a bad bet regardless of its EV, because you won't survive to collect. Keep every commitment to a size where a loss is recoverable, so your edge has *time* to compound. The goal isn't to maximize this one play; it's to still be playing when the edge inevitably pays. Don't risk the match on a coin flip you didn't need.

### Fractional Kelly — hedge against your own uncertainty
**Cole:** but i'm not even sure how big my edge is
**Nova:** Then bet *less* than the math says — fractional Kelly. Full Kelly assumes you *know* your edge exactly; you don't, you're estimating, and over-estimating your edge and betting it full is how you blow up. So practitioners bet a fraction — half-Kelly cuts the risk of a brutal drawdown dramatically while keeping most of the growth. Applied to a game: when you're *unsure* you're ahead, commit conservatively; reserve the big commit for when the edge is clear. Sizing down for uncertainty isn't timid — it's correctly pricing the fact that your read might be wrong.

### Quantify the uncertainty instead of ignoring it
**Cole:** i just kind of go with my gut on how risky to play
**Nova:** Gut is fine as an input, but Kelly's real lesson is to *quantify* rather than wave at it: roughly, how big is my edge here, and how sure am I? Make the risk a *deliberate function of those two*, not a mood. "I'm probably ahead but not certain" → measured. "I'm clearly ahead with a clean read" → press. "I'm behind and need variance" → the big swing is correct *because* the safe line loses. Naming your edge and your certainty turns risk from a feeling into a decision. The number doesn't have to be exact; the *habit* of pricing it is the edge.
